Let’s keep talking taxes (sorry!)

I think you can save $ on your taxes.

There’s a tax trick that might be of use to you: if your medical bills are more than 7.5% of your Adjusted Gross Income in 2019, you may be able to deduct them.

Your Adjusted Gross Income is your household income minus any deductions, like student loan payments or mortgage interest.

A few things to know:

  • This only applies to therapy that counts as medical care, so you should talk to your therapist during your intake about your diagnosis. Things like marriage counseling may not count.
  • You'll have to itemize your deductions to do this. In 2019, you'll only save money if your medical expenses (plus your other deductions like loan interest and charitable contributions) add up to more than $12,200 for a single person, or $24,400 for a married couple.
  • I can’t know what all of your deductions might be, but I can tell you this: without additional deductions, your therapy bills are currently % of your Adjusted Gross Income.

Frequently asked questions

How does this work?

This calculator walks you through three ways to make therapy more affordable: sliding-scale fees, insurance options (even if your therapist doesn't accept insurance!), and tax deductions.

Do you save my data?

Nope. None of the forms here send your numbers anywhere beyond this page. We're just doing some math for you.

Is this calculator correct?

As I mentioned in the disclaimer, I’m not an expert! I am, however, currently paying for my own therapist with a mix of out-of-network insurance claims and a Flexible Spending Account. I wanted to show people how the sticker price for therapy isn’t necessarily what you’re stuck paying... and then I kept researching insurance and tax deduction combinations... and many hours later, this calculator was born.

Please think of this is an exploration of your options, and talk to your insurance and tax professionals for verified expert advice.

Should I go to therapy?

That’s a big question, and it’s completely up to you and your doctors! They’ll give you better advice than I can. But clearly I'm a fan, and I want to destigmatize getting help if you decide you want it.

This is still too expensive. Do I have other options?

This article on BuzzFeed actually does a great job of walking you through some creative options for getting your fees lowered—from pro-bono therapy, to Employee Assistance Programs. You have lots of options.
